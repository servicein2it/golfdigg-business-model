Part 1

How Golfdigg works

In plain language — what the business does, who it serves, and how it makes money.

00 · In plain terms

It's like Booking.com — but for golf tee times.

Strip away the jargon and Golfdigg does one simple thing: it connects golfers who want to play with golf courses that have empty tee times — and keeps a small cut of every round it fills.

The problem it solves

Empty slots, hard booking

Courses can't sell every tee time, and golfers found booking slow, pricey and hard to compare.

What Golfdigg does

Book any course, instantly

One app shows live tee times at 300+ courses — book and pay in seconds, often at a big last-minute discount.

How it makes money

A cut of each booking

Golfdigg keeps a 10–20% commission per round. The course fills a seat it would have lost; the golfer saves.

01 · What Golfdigg is

A two-sided marketplace for perishable tee times.

Courses carry high fixed costs and inventory that expires the moment a tee time passes. Golfdigg aggregates that inventory, prices it dynamically, and puts it one tap away — then takes a commission on every round.

The category

On-demand booking

An OTA-style aggregator for golf: browse, compare, book and pay in seconds instead of phoning the pro shop.

The mechanism

Yield management

Discount unsold and off-peak slots 30–80% to fill capacity — the airline/hotel playbook, applied to fairways.

The footprint

Nationwide, multi-channel

300+ courses across 40 provinces via iOS, Android, web, LINE and a 24-hour call centre.

Supply · Golf courses

Capacity that expires

  • High fixed cost, zero salvage — grounds & staff are paid whether the slot sells or not.
  • Weak weekday & off-peak demand while weekends overflow.
  • No dynamic pricing tools to clear last-minute inventory.
Demand · Golfers

Booking is friction

  • Phone-and-hope — calling each pro shop with no live availability.
  • Opaque, inconsistent pricing with no way to compare.
  • No last-minute channel for the ~50% who decide after work.

00 · Analysis · Gaps

What they're missing — the openings.

Business-model gaps

Where the model strains

  • Thin margins — reliant on a 10–20% commission, with little recurring revenue.
  • Discount dependence — deal-led demand draws price-sensitive, low-loyalty users and squeezes course margins.
  • Single-market — concentrated in Thailand, capping the addressable market.
Product & experience gaps

Where the product can be beaten

  • Transactional only — book-and-leave; no play, scoring or social layer to retain golfers.
  • Tourists underserved — limited multilingual, packaged & localized-payment experience.
  • Few fintech & B2B tools — no wallet / BNPL / group-pay, no productized yield SaaS for courses.
Validate before you build: these are analytical hypotheses from public information. Confirm with app-store reviews, golfer interviews and course-operator conversations before committing.
